Research

Published Project | 2019

the study of Modification of CCTO thin films
by addind Mn compound and their gas sensing properties

      Undoped and Mn-doped CCTO films containing 1, 3 and 5 wt% of Mn dopants were prepared by a cost effective sol-gel method and inspect its property by various of techniques such as XRD, FESEM, EDX and XPS. The films have predominantly the perovskite CCTO crystal phases with very small amount of TiO2 secondary phases. The observation by FESEM shows that films has granular size ranging from 25 to 100 nm. The cross-section of the films on silicon substrate is approximately 650 nm while the film thickness on the alumina substrate is uneven due to substrate surface. And the oxidation state of the Mn in the compound is confirmed to be Mn2+. This work purpose is to enhance the film gas detectability by doping Mn into the crystal structure. In this work, both undoped CCTO and Mn-doped CCTO thin films were tested for selective sensing to H2S gas relative to NH3, H2, NO2 and ethanol vapor. The best sensitivity (~110) towards H2S was found in 5.0 wt% Mn-doped CCTO film at operating temperature of 250 °C. When compared with undoped CCTO sensor and Mn dopants cause a dramatic improvement of the response towards H2S gas up to 10 times higher. The chemical reaction with H2S and some other gasses will be altered, in the result, more free electrons have been released from the reaction which decrease the electrical resistance of the film even more. So the efficiency of the film will be increase for gas-detecting purpose.

Senior Project | 2023

the impact of Bio-diesel on Diesel Vehicle
Thermal Efficiency and Soot Reduction

      The objective of this research is to study the effect on brake thermal efficiency and the reduction of soot generated from the combustion of diesel engines. By using biodiesel B100 compared to standard diesel B7 to reduce air pollution problems caused by PM2.5 and the greenhouse effect problem, using alternative energy from biofuels produced from plants helps reduce global warming problems from the growing process of palm trees, which can reduce the amount of carbon dioxide through photosynthesis. The study found that cars using biodiesel B100 have a higher brake specific fuel consumption than cars using diesel B7 because biodiesel B100 has a lower calorific value than diesel B7 fuel. However, cars using biodiesel B100 have brake specific energy consumption and brake thermal efficiency that are similar to those of cars using diesel B7 fuel. It was also found that using biodiesel B100 can reduce soot caused by engine combustion. This is because the oxygen atoms in the biodiesel molecule contribute to more complete combustion. The amount of soot was reduced by approximately 50 percent. The average fringe length of biodiesel B100 is shorter than that of diesel B7 by 13.7%. Additionally, the soot density in biodiesel B100 is lower than in diesel B7, approximately 7%.
